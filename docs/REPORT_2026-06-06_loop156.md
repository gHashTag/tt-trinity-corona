## Why

The single-layer result (loop 155, 0.13% flips) left one honest question: error from
a per-MAC defect can ACCUMULATE through layers, so a deeper network might amplify it.
Since the respin recommendation rests on "task-quiet," testing depth-robustness is
the natural stress on that conclusion -- and the only genuinely new, decision-relevant
measurement left after the single-layer number.

## Research (this loop)

The depth result refines the signal-vs-decision picture from loop 155. Accumulation
is real and visible: a second gf16 layer roughly doubles the argmax-flip rate
(0.13% -> 0.22%), because the hidden activations already carry the first layer's
rounding/halving perturbation when the second layer's near-ties are decided. But two
mechanisms keep it bounded rather than compounding geometrically: (1) ReLU zeroes
roughly half the hidden units, discarding the perturbation on every inactive unit
before it can propagate; (2) the perturbation is a sparse, mean-near-zero ULP-scale
error, so across a 15-term hidden sum it tends to average down rather than reinforce.
The net is a small additive bump in churn with no accuracy cost -- which is the
behaviour you want a numerical defect to have, and the reason a halved-product bug
that looks alarming at 11x MAC RMS is benign at the task level even with depth. For
the respin file this means the "fold the free fix in if a slot opens, but not an
emergency" recommendation is now backed by both a shallow and a deep trained model.
