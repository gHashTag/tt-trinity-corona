# Loop 130 Report -- 2026-06-03

## Summary

**Measured the end-to-end accuracy impact of the gf16_mul rounding-overflow defect on
the actual MAC primitive (executes the prior loop's Option B) -- turning "0.072% of
products halved" into the number a respin decision needs. Built two study variants of
the dot-product unit -- `gf16_dot4_v2` (gf16_v2_mul + gf16_v2_add, fully corrected)
and `gf16_dot4_b` (gf16_v2_mul + the original gf16_add, to isolate the mul fix) --
and ran the shipped `gf16_dot4` against both over 20000 representative near-1.0
4-term dot products vs an exact-rational reference (`test/impact_gf16_mul.py`).
Result: the shipped MAC path runs at NMSE 1.49e-4 (~1.22% RMS); fixing the mul alone
drops it to 1.29e-6 (~0.11% RMS) -- a ~115x NMSE / ~11x RMS improvement; the gf16
floor (both fixed) is 5.33e-7 (~0.07%). The mul defect DOMINATES the MAC error
despite hitting only 0.27% of dot products, because halving a term is a huge
per-term error. A surprising by-product: this also corrects the earlier claim that
the gf16_add imprecision is "avoided by near-1.0 workloads" -- it actually perturbs
56% of dots, just with tiny per-dot amplitude. Bottom line: a single one-bit-too-
narrow register degrades the shipped gf16 MAC by ~11x in RMS, which materially
strengthens the respin case; `gf16_v2_mul` is the verified drop-in fix.**

## Why

The prior loop found and staged-fixed the gf16_mul defect but only had a per-product
rate (0.072%). A respin go/no-go needs the workload-level number, not a per-op rate.
Measuring on `gf16_dot4` (the real MAC the tiles/mesh instantiate) with an exact
reference produces that number and, by running mul-fixed-only vs fully-fixed,
attributes the error between the two gf16 defects cleanly.

## Research (this loop, web available)

The instructive twist is footprint vs. magnitude: the add bug is high-frequency
(56% of dots) but sub-ULP per dot, while the mul bug is rare (0.27%) but halves a
term -- so an NMSE (squared-error) metric is dominated by the rare-but-large mul
defect, the opposite of what the affected-count suggests. Reporting only "% of dots
affected" would have mis-ranked them; the energy-weighted metric is what a numerical
workload actually feels. This is the standard lesson that error *rate* and error
*norm* are different questions (Higham, *Accuracy and Stability of Numerical
Algorithms*): a sparse catastrophic error usually beats a dense tiny one in any
2-norm-based downstream (NMSE, loss, activation). It also reinforces that the
representative input distribution matters -- the same defect measured on adversarial
inputs would read very differently; near-1.0 MAC operands are the honest regime.
