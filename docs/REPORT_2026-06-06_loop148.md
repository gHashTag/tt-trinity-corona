`trinity_router_2x2` does clean one-hot forward addressing (packet dst[27:26] ->

to ONE replicated source: there are no distinct simultaneous tiles to lose, and
the last-wins bug actually yields the desired single-source XOR accumulate (a
*correct* 4-tile XOR of four identical inputs would give 0). The
`attested_mask`/`all_attested` path is correct regardless (one register per bit).
`multi_tile_receipt_v2` folds all simultaneous contributions for future
distinct-tile reuse (explicitly NOT a drop-in under the current hookup). Also
fixed a stale, non-CI testbench (`tb_integration_mesh.v`) whose wrong local packet
macros made it silently mis-fail. Verified by `test/fabric_audit.py`, green on
both dies.

## Research (this loop)

The `multi_tile_receipt` bug is the textbook Verilog non-blocking-assignment
hazard: in one always block, several `x <= ...` to the same `x` do NOT
read-modify-write in sequence -- they all sample the OLD `x` and the
last-scheduled assignment is the one that survives, so the design silently
realizes "x <= old_x ^ t3" instead of "x <= old_x ^ t0 ^ t1 ^ t2 ^ t3." It is the
same archetype as the rest of this audit -- RTL that diverges from the contract it
states ("XOR fan-in of 4 tiles") -- but here a second design decision (tying all
four ports to one source) cancels the divergence on the shipped die, which is why
it needs the both-layers analysis to grade. The lesson reinforces loop 147's: a
contract violation is not automatically a silicon defect; you have to check how
the block is actually wired before grading severity, and an honest audit reports
"latent/benign" as carefully as it reports "active."
