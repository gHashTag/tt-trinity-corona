## Why

A ~11x MAC RMS figure can read as alarming; a respin decision needs to know whether
it actually changes outcomes. Argmax (the prediction) is the outcome for a classifier,
so measuring prediction-flip rate converts the numerical defect into the decision the
program actually cares about -- and it turns out to be modest, which is as important
to state as the RMS was.

## Research (this loop, web available)

The lesson is metric selection across the stack: the same defect reads as ~11x in a
2-norm (NMSE, which a rare-but-large error dominates) yet ~0.24% in a 0/1 decision
metric (argmax, which is insensitive to magnitude except near the top-2 boundary).
Neither number is wrong; they answer different questions, and a respin decision needs
the decision-metric one. This mirrors the well-known robustness of argmax
classification to per-element noise (a large literature on quantized/low-precision
inference: the prediction is governed by the top-logit margin, not the elementwise
error) -- which is exactly why low-precision MAC formats like gf16 are viable at all,
and why a sparse halving bug, though numerically loud, is task-quiet. The honest brief
states both so the reader isn't mis-calibrated by either alone.
