## Why

gf16 was the one rung left imprecise -- but it is the silicon-`[Verified]` rung, so
overwriting its source would break source<->die correspondence. The correct move
is a separate corrected module plus a quantified risk assessment, which (a) closes
the accuracy gap with a verified drop-in, and (b) gives a concrete basis for a
respin/no-respin decision rather than a vague "it's imprecise." This is genuine
engineering, not make-work: it converts a known latent defect into a measured,
fixed, decision-relevant artifact.

## Research (this loop, web available)

The interesting tension is provenance vs. correctness: for a taped-out part the
source of record must match the silicon, so the right response to a latent RTL bug
is not to "fix" the shipped file but to (a) preserve it, (b) provide a verified
corrected variant, and (c) quantify the exposure so the bug can be triaged. The
quantification is the substance: a "512 ULP" number is meaningless without the
distribution -- here the errors live in subtractive cancellation (catastrophic
cancellation, Goldberg) and in overflow (the all-ones-exponent / large-product
regime), neither of which the validated near-1.0 workload exercises, which is
exactly why the bug survived FPGA/MNIST validation. The same lesson recurs across
this arc: an independent exact reference plus a stress distribution that targets the
fragile regimes is what surfaces and bounds the defect.
